: : Male loneliness : :

June 21, 2025

There’s a peculiar weight that comes with being raised to believe you must be the provider. The reliable one. The one who solves, who endures, who holds steady while the world shifts around you. Strength isn’t optional — it’s the default. You don’t grow up learning to ask for help. Not out of pride necessarily, but because no one teaches you how. There’s simply no space for vulnerability in the script handed to us. You patch your own leaks, swallow your fears, and keep moving. Be useful. Be needed. Or quietly become invisible.

For a while, this bargain made sense. Being needed was survival. But something shifted. The world — rightfully — opened its doors to female empowerment, autonomy, self-sufficiency. But while women stepped out of their predefined roles, no one ever came back to untie men from theirs. No one told us, “You can stop proving your worth through productivity now.” Our usefulness became optional. And optional, over time, starts to feel like... disposable.

Women — at least from where I stand — are no longer raised with the same expectation of servitude. Not fully. Not like before. And good for them. But the same wasn’t offered to men. The expectation of provision still clings to us like old code that no one bothered to debug. Our worth remains tethered to output — economic, physical, emotional. You are what you can provide. If you can’t — or won’t — then what are you?

And when that foundation cracks, something dark creeps in. Some men drift toward corners of the internet built on resentment — incels, the manosphere, algorithmic echo chambers. Others retreat into rigid ideologies that promise to restore order, discipline, certainty — the rise of modern reactionary politics feeds on this hunger. Because these spaces, as toxic as they can be, offer something the broader culture often withholds: clarity. Direction. A sense that being a man still means *something*. Without that? You drift. Toward anger. Toward numbness. Toward an isolation you start mistaking for independence. You become your own island. Not out of choice — but out of survival.

And so, you do the only thing you were ever taught: solve it yourself. Build. Work. Produce. Provide — even if no one asked you to. Even if there’s no one to receive it. You keep moving, because stopping feels like death. Because stopping forces you to face that maybe — just maybe — you’ve been running on the wrong script this entire time.

It took me longer than I like to admit to realize that isolation doesn’t heal itself. That no amount of “fix it alone” will patch the hole loneliness leaves. And that’s why I’m here now — swimming against the current the only way I know how. Looking for help. Therapy. Guidance. Not because it feels natural — it doesn’t. Everything in me screams to keep pretending I’m fine. But I know where that road leads. I’ve seen it. I’ve felt it. This fight — the one against the creeping silence, against becoming obsolete — it doesn’t get won alone.

I’m still standing. Still trying to be useful — maybe not in the ways I was taught. Maybe not as a provider. But as someone. For something. Hopefully. And maybe, just maybe, that’s enough.
